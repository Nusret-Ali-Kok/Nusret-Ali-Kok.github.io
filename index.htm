Computational Fluid Dynamics For Nozzle Geometries

Alh. In addition to the mechanical work for my graduate project, I also developed a custom Python tool to optimize nozzle properties for fluid flow, and conducted and optimized multiphase CFD analyses to examine laminar flow nozzle behavior in atmospheric conditions.

This nozzle was designed to be capable of dispersing pesticides accurately without affecting non-target plants in a farming field. Drawing on prior experience with computational fluid dynamics (CFD) from my coursework, I aimed to achieve laminar flow to maintain fluid cohesion during travel to the target. Initial CFD simulations revealed circulation points at the lower right corner of the nozzle (which can be seen in the second video) that disrupted laminar flow due to factors including inlet pressure and velocity, nozzle geometry, entrance angle, and the dimensions of the inner tubes. These issues were addressed by 3D printing the nozzle with a grid-structured infill, creating much narrower, precise pathways that allowed the fluid to maintain laminar flow and improve dispersion accuracy.

Patent

Alh. As part of a two-person team, I developed a Portable 3D-Printed Device for Low-Cost Torque-Adjustable Tension, Fatigue, and Tensile Testing. The device underwent multiple iterative design cycles, and the final product was recognized by instructors and selected for a University Patent Application. The patent has then been paused in the Under Review Phase.

The idea behind the machine was to build accessible tension, fatigue, and tensile testing equipment for university students, as such machinery is typically expensive. By designing the device using 3D-printed components, students could download the files and print it themselves for use in coursework, whether for assembly, design exploration, or material testing. The first prototype, as seen on the accompanying visuals, included a metal plate on top to account for bending stresses, which was later replaced by a second pillar identical to the first on the lower half of the device. Testing specimens were also 3D printed with varying infill amounts, and the device incorporated a 3D-printed gearbox to increase the torque of a cheap Nema stepper motor.
